1. Scalars, Vectors, Matrices and Tensors

  • Scalars: scalar is just a single number.
  • Vectors: an array of numbers.
  • Matrices: A\mathbf{A}A matrix is a 2-D array of numbers, so each element is identiﬁedby two indices instead of just one.

This example matrix A\mathbf{A}A has 3 rows and 2 columns.

A=[510−201−1] \mathbf{A}=\left[\begin{array}{rr}{5} & {10} \\ {-2} & {0} \\ {1} & {-1}\end{array}\right] A=⎣⎡​5−21​100−1​⎦⎤​

A = np.array([[5,10],[-2,0],[1,-1]])
  • Tensors: In some cases we will need an array with more than two axes. In the general case, an array of numbers arranged on a regular grid with avariable number of axes is known as a tensor. We denote a tensor named "A" with this typeface: A\mathbf{A}A. We identify the element of A at coordinates (i, j, k) by writing Ai,j,kA_{i, j, k}Ai,j,k​.

What are the Differences Between a Matrix and a Tensor?

  • The components of a rank-2 tensor can be written in a matrix.
  • The tensor is not that matrix, because different types of tensors can correspond to the same matrix.
  • The differences between those tensor types are uncovered by the basis transformations (hence the physicist's definition: "A tensor is what transforms like a tensor").

3. Matrix Inversion

A\mathbf{A}A is full rank and square, we denote by A−1\mathbf{A}^{-1}A−1 the inverse of A\mathbf{A}A.

  • AA−1=A−1A=I\mathbf{A} \mathbf{A}^{-1}=\mathbf{A}^{-1} \mathbf{A}=\mathbf{I}AA−1=A−1A=I
  • (aA)−1=a−1A−1(a \mathbf{A})^{-1}=a^{-1} \mathbf{A}^{-1}(aA)−1=a−1A−1
  • (A⊤)−1=(A−1)⊤\left(\mathbf{A}^{\top}\right)^{-1}=\left(\mathbf{A}^{-1}\right)^{\top}(A⊤)−1=(A−1)⊤
  • (AB)−1=B−1A−1(\mathbf{A B})^{-1}=\mathbf{B}^{-1} \mathbf{A}^{-1}(AB)−1=B−1A−1

A†\mathbf{A}^{\dagger}A† is a pseudo-inverse of A\mathbf{A}A.

  • A†AA†=A\mathbf{A}^{\dagger} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{A}^{\dagger}=\mathbf{A}A†AA†=A

6. The Moore-Penrose Pseudoinverse

"摩尔－彭若斯广义逆" in Chinese.

Pseudo-inverse exists and is unique for any matrix A.

Sometimes the pseudo-inverse is called the Moore-Penrose inverse.
